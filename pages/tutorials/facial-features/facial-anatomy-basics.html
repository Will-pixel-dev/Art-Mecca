Facial Anatomy Basics

Learn the fundamental structure of the human face - proportions, planes, and key landmarks for realistic portraits.

35 min read
Dr. Kenji Tanaka
Updated Weekly
Beginner / Intermediate

Tools Required

  • Sketchbook or digital drawing software
  • Reference photos of faces (different angles)
  • Anatomy guide (optional)
Key landmarks of the human face - front and side views

1Understanding Head Proportions

The foundation of any portrait is getting the proportions right. The classic "rule of thirds" divides the face into three equal sections: hairline to brow, brow to bottom of nose, and nose to chin.

Pro Tip

The eyes are located halfway down the head, not at the top. Many beginners place them too high!

2The Loomis Method

The Loomis method is a popular approach for constructing the head from any angle. Start with a sphere for the cranium, then add the jaw and facial planes.

Key Steps

  • Draw a circle for the main skull
  • Add center line and eye line
  • Construct the jaw and cheekbones
  • Add the facial features

3Facial Planes and Asymmetry

The face is not flat - understanding the planes of the face helps with shading and creating three-dimensional form. The forehead, cheeks, nose, and jaw all have distinct planes that catch light differently.

Pro Tip

No face is perfectly symmetrical. Embracing slight asymmetry makes portraits more lifelike and natural.

4The Nose Structure

The nose is often misunderstood. It consists of the bridge, the bulbous tip, the nostrils (ala), and the septum. The nose has distinct planes that catch highlights and shadows.

Nose Landmarks

  • Bridge (nasal bone)
  • Tip of the nose
  • Nostril openings
  • Nasal septum

5The Mouth and Lips

The mouth is defined by the horizontal opening, the two lips (upper and lower), and the corners (commissures). The upper lip typically has a distinct "Cupid's bow" shape.

Pro Tip

The mouth sits approximately halfway between the nose and the chin. The corners of the mouth align with the pupils.

6The Jaw and Chin

The jawline defines the lower border of the face. The angle of the jaw varies greatly between different face shapes - round, square, oval, and heart-shaped faces each have distinct jaw characteristics.

Face Shapes

  • Round: Soft, curved jawline
  • Square: Strong, angular jaw
  • Oval: Balanced proportions
  • Heart: Narrow chin, wider forehead

7Skull Anatomy for Artists

Understanding the underlying skull is the secret to drawing convincing faces. The skull defines the structure of the face - eye sockets, cheekbones, jawbone, and teeth all affect surface appearance.

Key Skull Landmarks

  • Orbital cavities (eye sockets)
  • Zygomatic arch (cheekbones)
  • Mandible (jawbone)
  • Nasal cavity
  • Maxilla (upper jaw)

8Aging and Facial Differences

Understanding how faces change with age, gender, and ethnicity will allow you to draw diverse, realistic portraits. Children have larger eyes and softer features, while older faces have more prominent bone structure and wrinkles.

Pro Tip

Study facial proportions across different ages and ethnicities to build a diverse reference library.

9Putting It All Together

Now that you understand the individual components, it's time to bring them together. Practice constructing complete faces from memory, using reference, and developing your own stylized approach.

Practice Tips

  • Draw from life whenever possible
  • Study master portraits
  • Do quick 5-minute gesture portraits
  • Focus on one feature at a time
